Flight Tournament

The Final Result

Air China CA923 stands out for receiving the highest score: 91.5665. Is it necessarily the best flight? What makes it better than CZ8309 (90.5)? If it is really the best flight, shall we all go for this flight when travelling between Shanghai and Tokyo? But if it is not, what are our data and visualizations for? For these reasons, our final result is not to declare which flight should be the first place, nor is it about any other calculations that might attribute to our flight tournament. Rather, in this section we reflect on our methodology, provide a more reasonable interpretation of our data, and, most importantly, discuss our thoughts on the epistemic function of data.

Though in round one and round two we filter off nearly half of our 23 candidates, the result of our tournament mostly comes from the calculations in round 3. Grading the performance of each flights in multiple aspects is a seemingly effective way to make the incommensurable comparable. However, our grading criterias and the weights of each factors are not objective. Though we tried our best, by referring to commensense, to adjust our grading criterias such that they can, in a sense, reflect how majority evaluates each factors, it is impossible to make our grading criteria reliable and objective. Or, at least an objective and reliable grading criteria is unattainable for us. Given that, our methodology should never be considered reliable. Instead, it is merely acceptable and can be utilized in our project.

Given that our methodology is not reliable and must be considered an expediency, what is the right way to interpret our data? We contend that any proposition regarding our data must be conditional. For example, suppose we want to interpret the score of CA923, the winner of our tournament. It makes no sense to say "CA923 is literally the best flight among all candidates. This is because it receives the highest score." Rather, it must be conditional. That is, only "if a passenger really cares about punctuality, namely the delay rate, he might want to consider CA923 for it receives the highest score under a grading criterias in which delay rate has a weight of 34% in total" should be considered a reasonable interprtation of our data. And this sheds light on the purposiveness of our data and visualizations. We contend that our data and visualizations, in this case, are not omnipotent. This is partly because that at this point we are still unable to fully cultivate the potential of data. Instead, our data and visualizations are merely supportive in a decision-making process. Our fighting tournament is, generally speaking, a decision-making process. And such process happens everyday at everywhere. In short, decision-making process is ubiquitous and hence significant for our life. And what we demonstrate is how data and visualizations might play a role in this important process. And our analysis shows us that our data and visualizations are not decisive for an objective grading standard is unattainable at this point. Yet, our data is helpful and illuminating for it presents a way by which we can rationally conduct a decision-making process to fulfil certain need or purpose.
